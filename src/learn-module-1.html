What is a prop firm?

Before you learn how to trade, it helps to understand why you're learning — and what a funded trading account actually means. This lesson explains the prop firm model in plain English.

The problem with trading your own money

Most people who want to trade face the same obstacle: capital. To make meaningful returns from trading, you need a meaningful amount of money to trade with. If you have £1,000 and make a 10% return in a month, you've made £100. That's impressive as a percentage but not life-changing as an income.

The maths only starts to work at scale. A 5% return on £100,000 is £5,000. The same skill, the same percentage — but a very different outcome.

This is where proprietary trading firms, or prop firms, come in.

Key concept

A prop firm provides capital to traders who can demonstrate disciplined, consistent trading. You trade their money, keep the majority of the profits, and they take a cut for providing the capital and absorbing the risk.

How the model works

Prop firms operate on a straightforward principle: they want to find traders who are consistent and disciplined, not lucky. To filter out gamblers from genuine traders, they run evaluation programmes — commonly called "challenges".

Why most traders fail challenges

The failure rate on prop firm challenges is high — estimates suggest only 10–20% of traders pass on their first attempt. The reasons are almost never about skill. They are almost always about behaviour.

The most common pattern is a trader doing well for two weeks, then having one bad day where they try to recover losses quickly and breach the drawdown limit. The challenge ends. The fee is gone.

This is why Traxent exists — to help you build the habits that prevent that pattern before you pay for a real challenge.

Common mistake

Many traders buy a challenge before they've practised the specific rules of that firm. Each firm has slightly different requirements around daily loss limits, consistency rules, and minimum trading days. Traxent tracks all of these so you know exactly where you stand before spending money.

This applies beyond day trading

The prop firm model is primarily associated with day traders — people making multiple trades per day on indices, forex, or futures. But the underlying principles of disciplined risk management, consistent behaviour, and capital efficiency apply to any form of active trading, including swing trading, options, and longer-term strategies.

Whatever your approach, the core lesson is the same: capital preservation comes first, profit follows.

How markets move

Markets can look chaotic, but they follow recurring patterns driven by human behaviour. This lesson covers the basics of price action — what's actually happening when a chart moves up or down.

Price reflects decisions, not just data

Every movement in a market price is the result of buyers and sellers agreeing — or disagreeing — on value. When more people want to buy than sell at the current price, price moves up. When more want to sell than buy, price moves down. Simple in theory. The complexity comes from the fact that millions of decisions are happening simultaneously, across different timeframes, driven by different information and motivations.

What you see on a chart is not just a line. It is a record of every trade that happened — the collective expression of confidence, fear, greed, and analysis from everyone participating in that market.

Key concept

Price action is the study of how price moves, without relying on external indicators. It asks: what is the market telling us right now, based purely on where price has been and where it is going?

The two forces: supply and demand

Every price movement comes down to the balance between supply and demand.

Demand zones are price levels where buyers have historically stepped in and pushed price up. When price returns to these levels, buyers tend to reappear — making them significant reference points.

Supply zones are the opposite — levels where sellers have pushed price down. These act as ceilings that price struggles to break through.

Real world analogy

Think of a market stall selling apples. At £1 each, demand is high and stock runs out quickly. At £3 each, buyers disappear and the seller is left with unsold stock. The market naturally finds a price where buyers and sellers agree — and that process of finding balance is exactly what you see on a trading chart.

Trends, ranges, and reversals

Markets spend most of their time in one of three states:

An uptrend is characterised by a series of higher highs and higher lows — each peak is higher than the last, and each pullback doesn't go as low as the previous one. A downtrend is the mirror image. A range is when price bounces between a ceiling and a floor without making progress in either direction.

Understanding which state the market is in is the first question any trader should ask before placing a trade. Trading against a strong trend is one of the most common causes of unnecessary losses.

What candlesticks tell you

Most trading charts display price using candlesticks — each candle represents a specific period of time (one minute, one hour, one day) and shows four pieces of information: the opening price, the closing price, the highest price reached, and the lowest price reached in that period.

A green (or white) candle means price closed higher than it opened — buyers won that period. A red (or black) candle means price closed lower — sellers won. The thin lines above and below the candle body are called wicks, and they show how far price moved in each direction before reversing.

Why this matters for prop firms

Prop firms evaluate you on consistency — not on whether you can predict the market perfectly, but on whether you understand what the market is doing and make sensible decisions based on that. A trader who understands market structure will avoid trading against strong trends, wait for clear setups, and manage risk appropriately. That is exactly the trader a prop firm wants to fund.

Timeframes — the same market, different perspectives

The same instrument looks very different depending on what timeframe you're viewing. A stock might be in a clear downtrend on the daily chart but show a short-term upward move on the 5-minute chart. Neither is wrong — they're just different zoom levels of the same picture.

Most professional traders use multiple timeframes — a higher timeframe to understand the overall direction and context, and a lower timeframe to find precise entry points. A common approach is to only trade in the direction of the higher timeframe trend, using the lower timeframe to time the entry.

For day traders working toward prop firm challenges, the 15-minute and 1-hour charts are commonly used for context, with entries taken on the 1-minute or 5-minute chart.

Your first risk calculation

Risk management is not optional — it is the single most important skill in trading. This lesson shows you how to calculate exactly how much to risk on any trade, before you enter it.

The number one reason traders lose money

It is not bad analysis. It is not bad luck. It is poor risk management. A trader with a mediocre strategy but excellent risk management will outperform a trader with a brilliant strategy and poor risk management over the long run — every time.

The reason is simple: losses compound just as quickly as gains. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain just to get back to where you started. The maths of loss recovery is brutal, which is why preventing large losses is more important than chasing large gains.

−10%
Requires +11% to recover
−25%
Requires +33% to recover
−50%
Requires +100% to recover

The 1% rule

The most widely used starting point in risk management is the 1% rule: never risk more than 1% of your total account on a single trade. This means if you have a £10,000 account, the maximum you should be willing to lose on any one trade is £100.

This might sound overly cautious, but consider the maths. If you follow the 1% rule and have 10 losing trades in a row — an unlikely but possible scenario — you've lost approximately 10% of your account. That is recoverable. If you risk 10% per trade and have 10 losers in a row, you've lost your entire account.

Key concept

Risk per trade is not the same as position size. Your risk is defined by your stop loss — the point at which you accept the trade has not worked and exit. Position size is calculated from your risk amount and your stop loss distance.

How to calculate position size

The formula is straightforward:

Position size calculation
Step 1
Define your risk
Account × risk % = £ at risk
Step 2
Set your stop
Price distance to stop loss
Step 3
Calculate size
Risk ÷ stop distance = units
Worked example

Account: £10,000 · Risk: 1% = £100 · Entry: 5,200 · Stop loss: 5,190 · Distance: 10 points · Position size: £100 ÷ 10 = £10 per point. If price hits your stop, you lose exactly £100 — your predefined risk.

Risk to reward — why win rate is not the whole story

Many new traders focus entirely on their win rate — how often they are right. But a trader who wins 40% of their trades can still be highly profitable if their winners are significantly larger than their losers.

This is the concept of risk to reward ratio (R:R). If you risk £100 on a trade but your target is £200 profit, your R:R is 1:2. At a 1:2 R:R, you only need to win 34% of your trades to break even. At 1:3, you only need to win 25%.

How this connects to prop firm rules

Every prop firm sets a maximum daily loss limit — typically 4–5% of the account. This is not arbitrary. It is designed to prevent one bad day from destroying weeks of good work.

If you are risking 1% per trade and your daily loss limit is 5%, you can have five losing trades in a row before hitting the limit. If you are risking 3% per trade, two bad trades end your day. The relationship between your risk per trade and the firm's daily limit defines how much room you have to be wrong — which you will be, regularly, even as a skilled trader.
