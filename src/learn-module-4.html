Module 4 · Lesson 1

FTMO decoded

FTMO is one of the most established prop firms in the world. This lesson breaks down every rule in practical detail — not just what the rules are, but where traders consistently trip up and how to structure your challenge so you don't need to rely on luck.

The FTMO evaluation structure

FTMO uses a two-step evaluation. Phase 1 — the Challenge — requires a 10% profit target. Phase 2 — Verification — requires 5%. Both phases share the same loss limits. Importantly, there is no time limit on either phase. You can take as long as you need, which removes one of the biggest failure triggers: forcing trades near an artificial deadline.

The floating loss rule — most misunderstood

Most traders understand the 5% daily limit in concept. What catches people is how it's calculated: FTMO measures your equity including unrealised floating losses on open positions, from your account balance at midnight Prague time (CET/CEST).

This means: if you start the day at £100,000 and open a position that goes −£5,100 floating — even though you haven't closed the trade — you've breached the daily limit. The trade is still live. The challenge is over. Even if it later recovers.

The floating loss trap

If your stop loss is correctly placed and sized at 1% risk, a single trade can never breach the daily limit on its own. The trap only catches traders who trade too large, move stops wider, or don't use stops at all. Proper position sizing from Module 3 is your protection.

Static drawdown — why it gets easier over time

FTMO's maximum loss limit is static: on a £100,000 account, the floor is always £90,000. No matter how much profit you make, the floor never moves up. This is a significant advantage — as your account grows from trading profits, the buffer between your current balance and the floor gets larger, not smaller. Once you're at £105,000, you have £15,000 of buffer above the £90,000 floor.

The 1-step FTMO — the Best Day rule

FTMO also offers a 1-step challenge. It is faster but adds an additional constraint: your single most profitable day cannot exceed 50% of your total Positive Days' Profit. This prevents traders passing via one lucky news event. For traders with consistent day-to-day performance, the 1-step can be faster. For traders with variable daily results, the 2-step is more forgiving.

The critical difference — static vs trailing drawdown

FTMO's drawdown is static: the floor is set at the start and never moves. This means the buffer between your current balance and the floor grows as you profit. The risk of breaching actually decreases over time.

Apex uses an intraday trailing drawdown: the floor follows your peak equity in real time, including unrealised gains on open trades. If your account grows to £107,000 intraday on a trade that hasn't closed, the floor may have moved up. If the trade then pulls back, you're closer to the floor than you expected — even though your closed balance is still higher than when you started.

Apex trailing drawdown — the key implication

Never let a profitable trade run back to breakeven on an Apex account. The moment a trade shows £1,000 profit floating, the floor may have moved up by £1,000. Giving it back means moving £1,000 closer to a breach — even though your account balance hasn't changed from when you entered. Take profits consistently.

Building your trading plan

Everything in this course leads here. A trading plan is not a strategy — it is the framework within which your strategy operates. Without one, even a profitable strategy fails under the emotional pressure of a real challenge.

What a trading plan actually is

A trading plan is a set of pre-committed decisions made when you're calm and thinking clearly — so you don't have to make them in the moment when emotions are high. It covers what you trade, when you trade, how much you risk, when you stop for the day, and how you review your performance.

The key insight

The purpose of a trading plan is to remove decisions from the heat of the moment. If your plan says "stop after two losses in a day", you don't need to decide whether today's third setup is "different enough" to override the rule. The decision is already made. Discipline is easier when the rules are pre-set.
