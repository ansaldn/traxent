Module 6 · Lesson 1

The numbers that actually matter

Most traders track the wrong number: profit and loss. P&L is an outcome, not a cause. This lesson covers the handful of metrics that actually describe whether you have an edge — and lets you compute your own expectancy.

Win rate is not your edge

New traders obsess over win rate, but a 70% win rate with tiny winners and huge losers loses money, while a 35% win rate with large winners and small losers prints. What matters is the combination of how often you win and how big your wins are relative to your losses. That combination has a name: expectancy.

Key concept — expectancy

Expectancy is the average amount you can expect to win (or lose) per trade, in units of risk (R). The formula: (Win% × Avg win in R) − (Loss% × Avg loss in R). A positive expectancy means that, repeated enough times, the system makes money. A negative one means it bleeds — no matter how good any single trade felt.

Thinking in R

The cleanest way to measure trading is in R — multiples of the amount you risked. If you risk 1% and make 2%, that is +2R. If you risk 1% and lose it, that is −1R. Measuring in R removes account size and position size from the picture and lets you compare every trade on equal footing.

Two traders, same P&L, very different edge
Trader A
70% win · +0.5R / −1R
Expectancy: (0.7×0.5) − (0.3×1) = +0.05R
Trader B
40% win · +2.5R / −1R
Expectancy: (0.4×2.5) − (0.6×1) = +0.40R

Trader B wins less than half the time but has 8x the expectancy. Over 100 trades, Trader A nets +5R while Trader B nets +40R. Same markets — completely different outcome, driven by the size of winners relative to losers.

Sample size matters

A positive expectancy over 10 trades tells you almost nothing — variance dominates small samples. You need 50+ trades before the number means much, and you must keep risk consistent for R to be comparable. This is exactly the kind of consistency a prop firm is testing for.

Journaling that changes behaviour

A journal that only records entries and exits is a spreadsheet. A journal that records your reasoning and emotional state is a mirror — and it is the single highest-leverage habit a developing trader can build.

What to actually log

Price data is the least useful thing in a trade journal — your platform already has it. The value is in the context only you can capture: why you took the trade, whether it matched your plan, how you felt, and what you did when it moved. That is the data that reveals your leaks.

The 'plan match' column is the whole game

If you log only one extra field, log whether the trade followed your plan. Over a few weeks you can sort your trades into four boxes — and the pattern that emerges is almost always the same.

The most dangerous box

Off-plan trades that win are the ones that destroy traders, because the profit teaches your brain that breaking the rules pays. The journal is what lets you label that win as a bad trade and refuse to repeat it. Without the record, you just remember the money.

Module 6 · Lesson 3

The weekly review loop

Logging trades is input. The review is where the learning happens. This lesson gives you a repeatable weekly routine that turns a pile of trades into one or two concrete changes — the loop that separates traders who improve from traders who just accumulate screen time.

Why weekly, not daily

Daily review is too noisy — a single day is mostly variance. Weekly gives you enough trades to see a pattern without so many that the signal blurs. The aim of each review is modest and specific: leave with one thing to keep doing and one thing to change. Not ten. One of each is enough to compound.

Make changes one at a time

If you change five things at once and your results improve, you have no idea which change helped — and you cannot keep what worked. Change one variable, give it a meaningful sample of trades, then measure. This is the same discipline a scientist uses, and it is why traders who review methodically pull away from those who tinker constantly.
