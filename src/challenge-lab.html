Week 1 · Lesson 1

The emotional map

Every blown challenge has an emotional signature. This lesson maps the five states that destroy disciplined trading — so you can recognise them in yourself before they cost you a challenge fee.

Why psychology matters more than strategy

You can have a profitable strategy, know the rules of your chosen firm, and understand risk management perfectly — and still fail a challenge. The reason is almost always psychological. The rules are easier to know than to follow when real money, even a challenge fee, is on the line.

The five emotional states below are responsible for the vast majority of prop firm challenge failures. They don't announce themselves clearly — they disguise themselves as rational decisions.

The five states — click each to understand it

Overconfidence
Dangerous
›
After a winning streak, risk management loosens
A trader has three winning days in a row. They feel invincible. They start taking trades that don't quite meet their criteria. They increase position size — "I'm on a roll." The fourth day wipes out all three days of gains. Overconfidence is most dangerous precisely when it feels most justified.
Fear
Dangerous
›
After losses, good setups are avoided entirely
After a series of losses, fear causes a different problem — a trader stops taking valid setups. They watch a perfect bull flag form and don't enter, convinced it will fail like the last three trades. The trade runs 3R without them. Fear creates a cycle where the trader's theoretical edge never gets expressed in practice.
FOMO
Dangerous
›
Chasing moves that have already happened
Price moves sharply without the trader. Instead of waiting for the next setup, they jump in late — buying near the top of the move, far from any structural stop level. Their stop has to be wide to make sense, increasing risk. Or they place a tight stop that gets hit immediately by retracement. FOMO trades almost never work because the entry is defined by emotion, not structure.
Revenge trading
Most dangerous
›
Increasing size after losses to recover quickly
The most common cause of blown challenges. A losing trade triggers the desire to recover immediately. The next trade is taken larger than usual — not because of a better setup, but to make back the loss faster. This trade also loses, and now the loss is compounded. The spiral accelerates. Within a session, a week of careful progress is erased.
Despair
Subtle
›
Giving up discipline when the challenge feels lost
When a challenge looks unreachable with time running out, some traders give up on their plan entirely — taking reckless trades to either "save" the challenge with a big win or simply stop caring. Despair is subtle because it can look like rational analysis: "I can't reach 10% in 5 days at 1% risk, so I need to take more risk." This logic is exactly wrong. One reckless trade can fail a challenge that was still technically recoverable.
Discipline
The goal
›
Process-focused regardless of recent results
A disciplined trader evaluates each trade on its own merits — not in the context of the last trade or this week's P&L. They follow their plan exactly, including stopping when personal limits are hit. They feel the emotions (everyone does) but don't act on them. Discipline isn't the absence of emotion — it's the ability to act correctly despite it.
The recognition exercise

After every trading session, ask yourself: which of the five states was present today? Being able to name the emotional state is the first step to managing it. Keep a one-line note in your sim journal each day identifying the dominant emotional state during that session.

Your personal failure pattern

Every trader has a dominant failure pattern — the emotional state they're most vulnerable to. This lesson helps you identify yours so you can build specific defences against it.

Why one pattern dominates

Most traders don't suffer equally from all five emotional states. One tends to dominate based on personality, past experiences, and the specific way they relate to risk and loss. Identifying your dominant pattern lets you build targeted defences rather than generic "be disciplined" advice.

Pre & post trade routines

Professional traders don't rely on willpower to stay disciplined. They build routines that make the right behaviour automatic. This lesson gives you two checklists — one to run before you enter any trade, one to run after any session.

Why routines work

Willpower depletes. A trader who relies on willpower to follow their plan will eventually fail — especially later in a session when fatigue and accumulated decisions have eroded their capacity for clear thinking. Routines bypass this by converting decisions into habits.

The pre-trade checklist is not about slowing you down — it takes less than 30 seconds once memorised. Its purpose is to insert a moment of deliberate evaluation between the emotional impulse to trade and the actual entry.

The pre-trade checklist

Pre-trade checklist — run this before every entry
Check 1
Structure
Is the daily structure aligned with this trade direction?
Check 2
Pattern
Is there a specific pattern I can name from my plan?
Check 3
Confluence
Do I have at least 3 independent reasons for this trade?
Check 4
Risk
Is my position size at or below my plan's maximum?
Check 5
Daily limit
If this trade loses, will I still be within today's personal limit?
Check 6
Emotional state
Am I entering this trade for the right reasons or to recover a loss?

If any check produces a "no" — do not enter. Wait for the next setup.

The post-session review

Post-session review — run this after closing the platform

Week 2 · Lesson 1

High-impact events decoded

NFP, FOMC, CPI — these acronyms drive the biggest market moves of the month. This lesson explains what each event measures, why markets react the way they do, and what the typical price behaviour looks like on a chart.

Why news events matter for prop traders

News events create the largest and fastest price moves of the trading month. For prop firm traders this cuts both ways — they're opportunities for significant gains, but they can also cause the fastest drawdown you'll ever experience. A single NFP release can move ES1! 30–50 points in seconds.

Most prop firm challenges allow news trading. A few ban it or restrict it. Understanding both the market behaviour and your specific firm's rules is essential.

The big three events

High-impact events — what they measure and why they move markets
NFP — Non-Farm Payrolls
Monthly job creation
Released first Friday of each month at 13:30 UTC
Why it moves markets
Employment = economic health = Fed policy expectations
Strong NFP → risk on, equities higher. Weak NFP → risk off.
FOMC — Fed Decision
Interest rate decision
8 times per year, Wednesday 19:00 UTC
Why it moves markets
Rate changes affect cost of capital for every asset class
Often more volatile than expected even when outcome is known.
CPI — Inflation
Consumer price index
Monthly, typically second week, 13:30 UTC
Why it moves markets
Inflation data drives Fed rate expectations
Hot CPI → rate hike fears → equities fall. Cool CPI → rally.

Typical news price behaviour

News events almost always produce a predictable sequence: spike → retrace → continuation (or reversal). The initial spike is driven by algorithmic reactions to the headline number. The retrace happens as traders evaluate the full report context. The subsequent move reflects the genuine market consensus.

For most traders, the safest approach is to avoid trading the initial spike and wait for the retrace to form a structural setup. The move after the retrace is cleaner, more predictable, and has a definable stop loss — unlike trading the spike itself where slippage and spread widen dramatically.

ES1! — watch how it reacts to news events
1H or 15M · Look for sharp spikes followed by consolidation

Prop firm news rules

Different firms have different rules around news trading. Getting this wrong can invalidate trades or breach rules you didn't know existed. This lesson covers what each major firm actually requires.

FTMO — no news restrictions on standard account

FTMO's standard challenge account has no explicit news trading ban. You can hold positions through NFP, FOMC, and CPI without restriction. However, the daily loss limit still applies in real time — a position held through a high-impact event that moves 40 points against you while your position size is too large can breach the daily limit in seconds.

The FTMO Swing account additionally allows overnight and weekend positions, making it more compatible with trading around weekly and monthly events.

Apex — no news ban, but trailing drawdown risk

Apex has no news trading ban. However, the intraday trailing drawdown creates a specific risk during news events. If a trade runs strongly in your favour during a news spike, the trailing floor rises with it. If the news reversal then takes it back, you may find yourself closer to the floor than you were before the event — even if you end up with a smaller profit than the peak.

MFFU — no news ban

MyFundedFutures has no news trading ban on its standard accounts. The end-of-day trailing drawdown means intraday spikes don't affect the floor until the session closes — giving slightly more tolerance for intraday news volatility.

Always verify current rules

Prop firm rules change. Some firms introduce news windows (periods where trading is suspended around high-impact events) without much notice. Check your firm's FAQ or terms before any major news event. Traxent is not affiliated with any firm listed here.

The universal news rule for prop traders

Universal approach regardless of firm
Before the event
Close open positions
Eliminate exposure to the uncontrollable spike
During the event
Do not trade
Wait for the initial spike and retrace to complete
After the event
Wait for structure
Trade the post-news setup once a clear level forms

Building your news filter

A news filter is a simple decision framework that tells you what to do before, during, and after each type of economic event. Once built, it removes the need to make judgment calls in real time.

The three-tier filter

News filter framework
Tier 1 — Red folder (NFP, FOMC, CPI)
No open positions
Close all positions 15 min before · Do not trade for 15 min after
Tier 2 — Orange (GDP, retail sales, PPI)
Reduce size
Halve position size if holding through · Widen stops slightly
Tier 3 — Low impact
Trade normally
Minor events unlikely to cause significant moves — normal approach applies

Mark your calendar at the start of each week with all Tier 1 and Tier 2 events. This takes 5 minutes and eliminates the possibility of being caught in a news event you didn't know was happening.

Moving to breakeven

Moving your stop to breakeven after a trade moves in your favour sounds like pure upside — a free trade. But done wrong, it kills more good trades than it saves. This lesson gives you the specific conditions that justify moving a stop.

The problem with moving to breakeven too early

The most common mistake: a trade moves 0.5R in profit and the trader immediately moves the stop to breakeven. Price retraces slightly — as it almost always does — and stops out at breakeven. The trade then runs to the original 2R target without them.

Moving to breakeven too early converts a good trade into a scratch trade. Done repeatedly, it destroys the R:R of your overall approach — you're taking 1:2 trades on paper but actually achieving 1:0.3 in practice because most runners get scratched before hitting target.

Partial profits & scaling out

Taking partial profits at key levels is one of the most practical trade management techniques for prop firm challenges. It locks in real gains, reduces psychological pressure, and lets part of the position run to a larger target.

How scaling out works

Instead of closing your entire position at one target, you close portions at multiple targets. The most common approach: close 50% at 1R, move stop to breakeven on the remainder, and let the remaining 50% run to 2R or 3R.

Scale-out example — £100 risk, 1:2 target
Full position exit at 2R
+£200 if wins
−£100 if stops out · All or nothing
Scale out: 50% at 1R, 50% at 2R
+£150 if both hit
+£50 if only first target hit then stopped · Smoother

The scale-out approach delivers a lower maximum gain (£150 vs £200) but a much better worst-case scenario if the trade reaches 1R then reverses. In prop firm challenges — where protecting what you've built matters — this smoothing effect is valuable.

The challenge-specific benefit

In a challenge, you're managing two numbers simultaneously — profit toward target and loss away from the floor. Scaling out locks in partial profit that counts toward the target and simultaneously reduces exposure. It's not just about psychology — it's mathematically better for challenge-style trading.

When scaling out hurts

If your win rate is low and you rely on large runners to compensate, scaling out reduces those runners. A trader with 30% win rate targeting 1:4 R:R should not scale out — they need the full 4R on every winner to make their system work. Scale-out suits traders with moderate win rates (40–55%) who want smoother equity curves.

When trades go wrong mid-session

Not every trade goes cleanly to stop or target. Conditions change mid-trade — news drops, the pattern fails before your stop is hit, session close approaches with an open position. This lesson gives you a decision framework for each scenario.

The three mid-trade scenarios

Mid-trade decision framework
Scenario
Pattern fails early
Price moves against you but stop not yet hit
Decision
If the setup reason is gone, exit early at a smaller loss
Don't hold hoping for recovery — the logic is invalidated
Scenario
News drops unexpectedly
Unscheduled event causes spike against position
Decision
Close immediately if daily limit is at risk
Protect the challenge over any individual trade
Scenario
Session close approaching
Open losing position with 30 minutes to session end
Decision
Close before the session ends — don't carry overnight
Unless your plan explicitly allows overnight holds
The hardest scenario

A trade moves against you but the stop hasn't been hit. The pattern is clearly broken — price has reclaimed a level it shouldn't have. The temptation is to hold and hope the stop holds. The disciplined action is to exit early at a smaller loss, freeing capital and mental energy for the next setup. Accepting a 0.5R loss voluntarily is far better than waiting for a full 1R loss on a trade whose logic no longer holds.
