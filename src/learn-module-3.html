Position sizing in depth

You were introduced to position sizing in Module 1. This lesson goes deeper — showing you how to apply it across different account sizes, instruments, and risk tolerances using real current prices from a live chart.

The formula revisited

The position sizing formula from Module 1: Position size = Risk amount ÷ Stop distance. Simple in principle. The complexity comes from applying it correctly across different instruments where the price of a "point" or "pip" varies significantly.

For forex: a pip on EUR/USD at standard lot size equals approximately $10. For futures: a point on ES1! equals $50 per contract. For CFDs: varies by instrument and broker. The formula is the same — the units differ.

Scaling risk with account size

The 1% rule is a starting point, not a rigid rule. As your account grows and your consistency improves, some traders scale to 1.5% or 2% per trade. The key is that your risk per trade relative to the daily loss limit should leave you with enough buffer to weather a bad day.

Key principle

Always calculate your position size before you enter a trade — never after. The calculation should confirm your size, not justify it. If the correct size feels too small, the trade might not be worth taking.

Stop loss placement

Where you place your stop loss is as important as where you enter. A stop placed too tight gets hit by normal market noise. A stop placed too wide means you're risking too much. This lesson covers the principles of logical stop placement.

The purpose of a stop loss

A stop loss has one job: to exit the trade at a predetermined price when the reason you entered is no longer valid. It is not there to prevent losses — losses are part of trading. It is there to ensure that any single loss is controlled and predictable.

The worst type of stop loss is one placed at a round number or at a fixed point distance from entry without any relationship to the market structure. A good stop loss is placed at a level where, if price reaches it, the original trade idea is clearly wrong.

Key concept

Your stop loss should be placed at the point where the market tells you your trade idea is wrong — not at the point where your pain tolerance runs out.

Three logical stop loss placements

Logical stop loss locations
Below swing low
Structure based
Below the low that defined the pattern entry
Below mother bar
Pattern based
For inside bars — below the mother bar low
Below VWAP
Level based
For VWAP reclaim entries — below the reclaim candle

The buffer principle

Markets are not precise. Price often moves a few points beyond a level before reversing — this is called a wick or a liquidity sweep. Placing your stop exactly at a swing low means a single wick can stop you out even if your trade direction is correct. A small buffer — 1-3 points or pips beyond the level — helps avoid being stopped out by noise while still exiting if the level is genuinely broken.

Prop firm specific

On futures accounts with trailing drawdown (Apex, MFFU), be especially careful about stop placement. A wide stop combined with multiple positions can consume your trailing drawdown quickly on a volatile day. Tight, logical stops protect both your trade and your drawdown buffer.

Risk to reward — the long game

Understanding risk to reward is what separates traders who think about each individual trade from traders who think about their edge over hundreds of trades. This lesson shows you why a 40% win rate can be more profitable than a 70% win rate.

Expectancy — what matters over time

Every trader has a win rate (how often they are right) and an average risk to reward ratio (how much they make per winner relative to how much they lose per loser). These two numbers together determine whether a trading approach is profitable over time — regardless of any individual trade result.

The formula: Expectancy = (Win rate × Average win) − (Loss rate × Average loss). If this number is positive, the approach is profitable over time. If it's negative, no amount of good luck makes it sustainable.

Setting realistic profit targets

Profit targets should be set at the next logical level of resistance — not at an arbitrary 2:1 or 3:1 ratio. If the next resistance level is only 1.2× your risk, the trade has a poor R:R and should be skipped or have the stop tightened. If the next resistance is 4× your risk, you have an excellent setup.

How accounts blow up

Understanding the specific ways trading accounts get destroyed is just as important as knowing how to build them. This lesson covers the four most common account killers — all of which are preventable.

The four account killers

In almost every prop firm challenge failure, one of these four patterns is present. They're not about strategy — they're about psychology and discipline.

The four account killers
1. Revenge trading
Emotional response
Taking bigger trades after a loss to "get it back"
2. Overtrading
Too many trades
Taking setups that don't meet your criteria
3. Moving stops
Breaking the rules
Moving stop loss further away when trade goes wrong
4. No daily loss limit
No self-control
No personal rule to stop trading after losing X%

The revenge trading spiral

Revenge trading is the single biggest cause of prop firm challenge failures. The pattern is consistent: trader loses a trade, feels frustrated, increases position size on the next trade to recover quickly, loses again, increases further, blows the daily loss limit. The entire sequence can happen in under an hour.

The solution is simple but hard to implement under pressure: set a personal daily loss limit below the firm's limit. If you lose 3% in a day, stop trading. Walk away. Come back tomorrow. The challenge has no time limit. The account does not care that you had a bad morning. Tomorrow is always a fresh start.

How professionals handle losses

Professional prop firm traders treat a losing day the same way a professional golfer treats a bad hole: they don't try to make it back on the next hole with a reckless shot. They play the next hole as they would any other — with the same process, the same criteria, the same discipline.

This is the mental model that separates traders who consistently pass challenges from those who pass once, then fail repeatedly.

The rule that saves most challenges

Set your personal daily stop-loss at 2.5–3% — well below the firm's 4–5% limit. If you hit it, stop for the day. This one rule prevents revenge trading from ending your challenge, and leaves you with enough buffer that a bad morning can't cost you everything.
