What is a prop firm?

Before you learn how to trade, understand what you're working toward. This lesson explains the prop firm model in plain English — including the specific rules you'll need to follow to pass a funded account evaluation.

The problem with trading your own money

Most people who want to trade face the same obstacle: capital. To make meaningful returns, you need a meaningful amount of money to trade with. If you have £1,000 and make a 10% return in a month, you've made £100. Impressive as a percentage — but not life-changing as an income.

The maths only works at scale. A 5% return on £100,000 is £5,000. Same skill, same percentage — very different outcome. This is where proprietary trading firms, or prop firms, come in.

Key concept

A prop firm provides capital to traders who can demonstrate disciplined, consistent trading. You trade their money, keep the majority of profits, and they take a cut for providing the capital and absorbing the risk.

How the evaluation works

To filter out gamblers from genuine traders, prop firms run evaluation programmes — commonly called "challenges". You pay a fee, trade a simulated account, and if you hit the profit target without breaking the rules, you receive a funded account.

The rules — click each one to understand it

Most failures come not from bad trading but from breaking these rules. Click each rule below to understand exactly what it means in practice.

Daily loss limit
5% / day
›
Your account equity — including unrealised losses on open trades — cannot drop more than 5% below your starting balance for that day. On a £100,000 account, equity must never fall below £95,000 at any point during the session, even on an open trade that hasn't been closed yet.
Maximum total loss
10% overall
›
Your account balance can never fall below 90% of its starting value — ever, across the whole challenge. This is a permanent static floor. On a £100,000 account, your equity must never touch £90,000. Unlike the daily limit, this doesn't reset — it's permanent for the duration of the challenge.
Consistency rule
30–50% max day
›
On funded accounts, some firms require that no single trading day accounts for more than 30–50% of your total profit. This prevents passing by getting lucky on one big news trade. It forces genuine consistency over time — which is exactly what prop firms want to see.
Minimum trading days
4–7 days
›
You must trade on a minimum number of days during the challenge, even if you hit the profit target early. An account that hits 10% profit on day one still can't pass — you need to prove you can trade consistently over multiple sessions, not just get lucky once.

Why most traders fail challenges

The failure rate on prop firm challenges is high — most estimates put it between 80–90% of first attempts. The reasons are almost never about skill or strategy.

The pattern that kills most challenges

A trader does well for two weeks, has one bad day, then tries to recover losses quickly by increasing position size. They breach the daily loss limit. The challenge ends. The fee is gone. This is why discipline matters more than strategy.

How markets move

Markets follow recurring patterns driven by human behaviour. This lesson covers the basics of price action — and you'll practise identifying market states directly on a real live chart.

Price reflects decisions, not just data

Every movement in a market price is the result of buyers and sellers agreeing — or disagreeing — on value. When more people want to buy than sell, price moves up. When more want to sell than buy, price moves down. What you see on a chart is the collective expression of confidence, fear, greed, and analysis from everyone participating in that market.

Key concept

Price action is the study of how price moves, without relying on external indicators. It asks: what is the market telling us right now, based purely on where price has been and where it is going?

The three market states

Markets spend most of their time in one of three states. Understanding which state you're in is the first question any trader should ask before placing a trade. Trading against a strong trend is one of the most common causes of losses — especially during a prop firm challenge when every loss counts against your drawdown.

What candlesticks tell you

Most trading charts display price using candlesticks. Each candle represents a time period and shows four pieces of information: the opening price, closing price, highest price, and lowest price reached during that period.

Anatomy of a candlestick

Start price
Where the period began
Close
End price
Where the period finished
High / Low
Extremes
Shown as wicks above and below

A green candle means price closed higher than it opened — buyers won that period. A red candle means price closed lower — sellers won. The wicks show how far price pushed before reversing.

Timeframes — same market, different zoom levels

The same instrument looks very different depending on your timeframe. A market in a clear downtrend on the daily chart might show a short-term upward move on the 15-minute chart. Neither is wrong — they're different zoom levels of the same picture.

For prop firm challenges, most traders use the 15-minute or 1-hour chart for context, and the 5-minute chart for precise entries.

Why this matters for prop firms

Prop firms evaluate you on consistency — not on predicting the market perfectly. A trader who understands market structure will avoid trading against strong trends, wait for clear setups, and manage risk appropriately. That is exactly the trader a prop firm wants to fund.

Your first risk calculation

Risk management is the single most important skill in trading. This lesson shows you how to calculate exactly how much to risk on any trade — and you'll practise the calculation using a live chart setup of your choice.

Why risk management beats strategy

A trader with a mediocre strategy but excellent risk management will outperform a trader with a brilliant strategy and poor risk management — every time. Losses compound just as quickly as gains, and the maths of loss recovery is brutal.

−10%
Requires +11% just to get back to where you started
−25%
Requires +33% to recover
−50%
Requires +100% to recover — nearly impossible

The 1% rule

Never risk more than 1% of your total account on a single trade. On a £10,000 account, your maximum loss per trade is £100. Ten consecutive losers only costs you ~10% — recoverable. Risk 10% per trade and ten losers wipes you out entirely.

Key concept

Your risk per trade is not the same as your position size. Risk is defined by your stop loss — the point at which you accept the trade hasn't worked. Position size is calculated from your risk amount divided by your stop loss distance.

Position size formula — step by step
Step 1
Define risk
Account × risk % = £ at risk
Step 2
Measure stop
|Entry − stop loss| = distance
Step 3
Calculate size
Risk £ ÷ stop distance = units

Risk to reward — why win rate isn't everything

Many new traders focus entirely on their win rate. But a trader who wins 40% of their trades can still be highly profitable if winners are significantly larger than losers. This is the risk to reward ratio (R:R).

Break-even win rate at different R:R ratios
R:R 1:1
50% needed
Win half your trades to break even
R:R 1:2
34% needed
Lose twice as often — still profitable
R:R 1:3
25% needed
Win only 1 in 4 — still profitable

How this connects to prop firm rules

Every prop firm sets a maximum daily loss limit — typically 4–5% of the account. If you risk 1% per trade, you can have four or five losing trades in a row before hitting the limit. If you risk 3% per trade, two bad trades end your day. The relationship between your risk per trade and the firm's daily limit defines how much room you have to be wrong — which you will be, regularly, even as a skilled trader.
